Transformed data from wide to long format:

An excellent R function is pivot_longer that transforms the wide format data to long format. The cols vector is passed as the parameter providing the target column names. names_to is the parameter that tells what will be the name of the column name in the long format where as values_to parameter tells what will be the column name for the values.

df <- df %>% pivot_longer(
    cols = c("Los Angeles", "Phoenix", "San Diego", "San Francisco", "Seattle"),
    names_to = "City",
    values_to = "Count"

# Step 1: Filter on-time flights and summarize by Airline and City
city_df <- df %>%
  filter(Status == "on time") %>%
  group_by(City, Airline) %>%
  summarise(
    Total = sum(Count),
    .groups = "drop"

  group_by(City) %>%
  mutate(
    Percentage = round((Total / sum(Total)) * 100, 2)

# Step 2: Convert to wide format

Discrepancy description between comparing two airlines’ flight performances city-by-city and overall:

The overall comparison shows that AM WEST has a higher proportion of on-time flights (66.41%) compared to ALASKA (33.59%), suggesting that AM WEST performs better. However, when the data is analyzed by city, the results vary significantly. ALASKA outperforms AM WEST in cities such as San Francisco (61.12% vs 38.88%) and Seattle (90.16% vs 9.84%), while AM WEST performs better in Los Angeles, Phoenix, and San Diego.

This discrepancy occurs because the distribution of flights across cities is uneven. AM WEST operates a large number of flights in Phoenix, where it has a very high on-time rate, which heavily influences the overall results. In contrast, ALASKA performs extremely well in Seattle but has fewer flights in cities where AM WEST dominates.

This is an example of Simpson’s Paradox, where aggregated data suggests one conclusion, but subgroup analysis reveals a different pattern. It highlights the importance of analyzing data at multiple levels before drawing conclusions.

Conclusion

The overall comparison suggests that AM WEST outperforms ALASKA in on-time arrivals; however, the city-by-city analysis reveals a more nuanced pattern where ALASKA performs better in several individual cities. This discrepancy indicates the presence of Simpson’s Paradox, where aggregated data can mask underlying trends within subgroups. The imbalance in flight distribution across cities, particularly AM WEST’s concentration in high-volume routes like Phoenix, skews the overall results. Therefore, performance should be evaluated at both the aggregate and city levels to avoid misleading conclusions.
